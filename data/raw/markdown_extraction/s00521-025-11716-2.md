## Abstract

Video surveillance has benefited greatly from advances in artificial intelligence, particularly in computer vision, and the number of monitored environments has consequently increased, creating new challenges in extracting relevant information. When multiple cameras cover adjacent areas, overlapping fields of view can cause the same subject to be detected across cameras, introducing duplicate counts. Although the literature offers established solutions, many rely on high-quality recordings and complex, computationally intensive methods, limiting their use on resource-constrained devices. In this work, we address these challenges with a solution that leverages minimal information about target subjects and uses a transductive strategy to detect duplicates based on interactions within overlapping fields of view. Experiments in real-world settings show that our approach suppresses duplicates effectively, making it suitable for deployment on resource-limited hardware. We evaluate state-of-the-art lightweight models with high inference speed, as well as classical re-identification methods, in scenarios with low-quality video and constrained devices. The results underscore the effectiveness of the proposed approach and motivate exploration of re-ID with domain adaptation, as well as anchor-free methods with weak or semi-supervised learning.

Keywords Multi-camera surveillance · Transductive learning · Duplicate removal · Deep neural network

## 1 Introduction

Video surveillance systems consist of cameras specifically configured to monitor activities in both public and private settings while subjects act naturally [ 1 ] . The adoption of such systems has been widely increasing to support several monitoring tasks such as security in general [ 2 ] , accident detection [ 3 ] , urban mobility [ 4 ] , personal protection equipments [ 5 ] , and costumer behaviors [ 6 ] . Due to the massive number of recorded scenes continuously monitored, it is imperative to use computer vision techniques to extract useful information automatically.

Another relevant challenge is the integration of multiple cameras in the same area, monitoring the same subjects [ 7 ] . In this situation, the recorded scenes may provide different perspectives and possibly duplicated data. Therefore, a relevant research question is the identification of duplicate targets to avoid redundancies and present concise information. To overcome this problem, it is possible to use well-known Deep Neural Network (DNN) models, including face recognition to detect the same person in different cameras as well as person re-identification techniques [ 8 – 12 ] . Besides using DNN, authors have also published related works focused on extracting distinctive invariant features from images to perform reliable matching between different views of objects and scenes [ 13 – 15 ] . These models have proven highly effective across various contexts; however, their performance can be significantly influenced by factors such as infrastructure limitations, video quality, subject positioning, and the distance between subjects and cameras. Furthermore, as emphasized in the literature, key challenges include handling occlusions, varying viewpoints, and changes in appearance over time–issues highlighted by recent studies published in top-tier computer vision conferences and journals over the past two years [ 16 – 20 ] . In such scenarios, beyond the computational cost of image processing, the feature vectors extracted from detected faces may vary substantially, even when representing the same individual.

In this work, we decided to tackle these problems from a different perspective, using fewer details about subjects and creating a semi-supervised approach using transductive learning to detect duplicates according to their interaction in overlapped fields of view captured by cameras. In a nutshell, our approach is based on a primary stage, which runs a supervised method to detect target subjects in multiple scenes recorded from different cameras. Secondly, subjects are mapped as points distributed on a space to be later analyzed by a regression method, whose outputs are used as delimiters to the transition regions. By using delimiters, one can identify overlapped regions and discard multiple detections for the same subjects. There are several situations where this issue occurs, such as counting cars on streets, people in stadiums and shows, animals in wild areas, and more. In this work, we have made some assumptions to assess our proposal carefully: (i) cameras are in fixed positions; (ii) no predefined positions are necessary for the cameras; (iii) high-quality videos are not required; (iv) subjects move freely in the monitored area; and (v) overlapped areas between cameras may exist, potentially showing duplicated subjects. In this context, we collected and analyzed videos recorded indoors with multiple cameras positioned to monitor people in overlapping fields of view.

The contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:

- • New approach to detect duplicated subjects in multi-camera monitoring;
• By combining classification and regression methods, fewer subject-specific details are required, eliminating
the need for high-quality recordings or complex models to perform face recognition;
• Our contribution is suitable for resource-constrained devices, such as those used in edge computing.
The remaining of this manuscript is organized as follows: we first introduce the problem statement; next, in Sect. 3 , we present our proposed approach; Sect. 4 shows the experimental setup and the obtained results; finally, in Sect. 5 , we draw conclusions and discuss future work.

## 3 Proposed approach

This section describes our proposed approach, which consists of two main stages, as detailed in Algorithm 1 . In the first stage, we train a supervised model to detect the target subjects, i.e., users who move freely through indoor and outdoor environments, specifically a computer laboratory and bus stations. The second stage focuses on automatically generating delimiters that highlight transition regions where user duplication is likely to occur.

Along with our contribution, we also present in this section a strategy using a DNN model trained to crop regions in images using predefined bounding boxes previously defined by specialists, thus explicitly removing overlapped fields of view. Our approach does not directly use this strategy; it was implemented as a baseline in our comparison.

### 3.1 First stage—face detection

The detection used in this stage depends on the problem under investigation (e.g., objects, cars, or animals) and can be modified according to the readers' needs. Once the focus of our investigation is on users, we could consider both person or face detection. We opted for face detection in this approach due to the presence of numerous objects in the environment that often occlude people, but leaving mostly their faces visible. All faces in the collected videos were manually annotated, resulting in 2,414 bounding boxes—approximately 600 faces per video. Next, we employed YOLO (You Only Look Once) [ 21 ] version 11, from the Ultralytics library, in our experiments. Table 1 summarizes the key architectural parameters of this model.

YOLO is a family of real-time object detection models known for treating detection as a single regression problem, directly predicting bounding boxes and class probabilities from full images in one evaluation. This unified and efficient approach makes YOLO particularly suitable for tasks requiring high-speed inference. As shown in Fig. 4 , YOLO models are generally organized into three major components: backbone, neck, and head.

The backbone, highlighted in blue in Fig. 4 (Backbone), refers to the portion of the network responsible for extracting general visual features from the input image. These features encode information about edges, textures, shapes, and more complex patterns as the network deepens. In YOLOv11, the backbone employs a module known as C3k2, which builds upon the Cross Stage Partial (CSP) design and uses small convolutional kernels to reduce computational load while preserving expressive power.

### 3.2 Second stage—semi-supervised field of view projection estimation

As previously discussed, the proposed approach begins by aligning frames from different cameras and detecting bounding boxes around each identified face to serve as coordinate references (Algorithm 1 — Lines 1 to 5). Next, the coordinates are modeled by regressors to create delimiters for the transition regions between fields of view (FOV) monitored by a set of surveillance cameras $S = \{s_1, s_2, \dots, s_k\}$ . Hence, considering a scenario with two cameras as stated in our case study, FOV for a camera $s_1$ is projected to recordings from $s_2$ , and vice versa. In summary, each delimiter works as a separator to avoid repeatedly considering the same subject in different cameras, i.e., the same target subject can be detected in multiple cameras; however, it is analyzed only once.

In practice, the subject detection in overlapping regions can present some imprecision caused by the deviations in the FOV lines or the parallax effect when the subject appears to have a different position relative to the background between the views of each camera. To attenuate this problem, in our case study, a good estimator for the number of users (U) can be obtained by $U = \sum_{i=1}^{n} D_i - E[O]$ , in which $D = \{d_1, d_2, \dots, d_i\}$ and $O = \{o_1, o_2, \dots, o_n\}$ are the total number of detected faces and detected faces inside the overlapping region for all cameras in S. If the analysis is computed between pairs of cameras and the presence of users inside their FOV has the same probability, the number of users is estimated by the difference between the sum of the difference detected and the expected users in FOV.

### 3.2.1 Initial setup and finding matches

Considering our case study, the red edges in the left and right blocks, illustrated in Fig. 2 , define how the cameras must be integrated into our proposed approach. To estimate the FOV delimiter on Camera $s_1$ , we monitor Camera $s_2$ , looking for detections of people entering the frame of Camera $s_3$ through one of its red edges (Algorithm 1—Line 6). When this happens, the person detected entering the frame of camera $s_2$ should also be present in the frame of camera $s_1$ , approximately along the FOV delimiter line of Camera $s_1$ that we want to estimate. Therefore, we want to record the coordinates of this person on Camera $s_1$ to use this information in the estimation process (Algorithm 1—Lines 7 to 11).

In the best-case scenario, when a person is detected entering the frame of Camera $s_2$ through one of its red edges, there will only be one person present in Camera $s_1$ , allowing us to confidently identify them as the same individual (Fig. 5 a). However, in typical conditions, there may be multiple people present in Camera $s_1$ (Fig. 5 b). Since no re-identification technique is employed, it is impossible to determine which individual corresponds to the person detected in Camera $s_2$ , leading to ambiguity. ### 3.3 Supervised baseline: area detection

According to the literature, in addition to face recognition, some authors address this problem using overlapping area detection. In our experiments, the proposed approach was evaluated by integrating the recorded areas from multiple cameras using Deep Neural Networks (DNNs). Similar to the face detection stage, we created bounding boxes to highlight regions of interest (ROIs) in all images from the right camera block, as illustrated in Fig. 8 . We then trained a DNN to identify similar ROIs in new video frames and to discard any faces detected outside these regions–assumed to be duplicates originating from the left camera block.

The main advantage of this approach is its simplicity, which makes it easily implemented in different contexts and infrastructures. On the other hand, its main disadvantages are the requirement for a specific ROI defined in advance for all application and the need to redefine and retrain the ROI when the camera position changes.

## 4 Results

The analyses performed to assess the proposed following conventional practices in the field of Computer Vision (CV) for object detection tasks, which is based on the Intersect over Union (IoU) ratio, $IoU = \frac{GrP}{IoU}$ . It essentially computes the performance by comparing the overlapped areas between an expected (ground truth) bounding box (G) and the predicted one (P).

During inference, the confidence $C$ of a bounding box is defined by $C = Pr(Class_i|object) \times Pr(object_i)$ . By choosing a threshold $\tau_1 \in [0, 1]$ for $C \geq \tau_1$ , we decide whether we should consider the predicted bounding box as a detected object from Class $i, i=1...N$ . By choosing a threshold $\tau_2 \in [0, 1]$ for IoU $\geq \tau_2$ , we can determine whether there is sufficient overlap between a predicted bounding box and a ground truth box of the same class to consider the detection a True Positive (TP). If a predicted bounding box does not sufficiently overlap any ground truth box (i.e., IoU $< \tau_2$ ), it is counted as a False Positive (FP). Conversely, if a ground truth object is not matched by any prediction with IoU $\geq \tau_2$ , it is considered a False Negative (FN).

After calculating the number of TP, FN, and FP, we can derive important measures such as Precision, Recall, and F1-Score. In summary, Precision assesses the exactitude of the model by computing the rate of correctly predicted detections over the number of predicted detections, while Recall evaluates the completeness of the model by computing the rate of correctly predicted detections over the number of elements expected to be detected. F1-Score represents a harmonic mean of Precision and Recall. The Average Precision (AP) is a metric that combines Precision and Recall values over a range of $\tau_1$ , given a fixed $\tau_2$ . Two other metrics, based on AP, that are widely used to compare performance between models are mAP@50 and mAP@50-95. The mAP@50 (mean Average Precision at IoU = 0.50) considers a detection correct if the IoU is at least 0.50, providing a smoother evaluation. In contrast, mAP@50-95 is a more comprehensive metric that averages AP across multiple IoU thresholds, from 0.50 to 0.95 in steps of 0.05, offering a more stringent and balanced assessment of detection accuracy.

According to the curve F1-score $\times$ confidence (Fig. 9a ), all models presented great performance varying from 0.87 to 0.91, showing no significant difference between them. A similar behavior was noticed in the Precision-Recall curve shown in Fig. 9b .

## 5 Final remarks

In this work, we address the challenging task of reducing the impact of duplicated information in multi-camera surveillance systems. This problem arises when the system monitors the same space, creating overlap in regions covered by two or more cameras. In the literature, there are well-known approaches to overcome this situation, such as subjects' re-identification to detect duplicates. However, the performance of such models can be significantly affected by factors like the analyzed target, video quality, and the distance between subjects and cameras.

Our contribution introduces an efficient and adaptable approach for automatically detecting overlapping fields of view (FOV) without relying on high-quality recordings or subject re-identification. By combining classification and regression methods, our method significantly reduces the need for detailed subject features, making it suitable for both indoor and outdoor environments and especially valuable in resource-constrained devices, such as those used in edge computing. The results demonstrate that our method achieves performance levels comparable to manually labeled models, while maintaining flexibility: if monitoring cameras are repositioned, the system automatically recalculates the FOV lines, ensuring adaptability to dynamic environments.

As highlighted in our experiments, yolo11n was approximately 30 times faster than yolo11x and achieved a higher F1-score after fine-tuning with our approach than all versions of YOLOv11 without fine-tuning, reinforeing the efficiency and accuracy of our detection pipeline. As future work, we plan to evaluate our approach using alternative regression methods. Although wrANSCAN offers a simple and effective solution, it operates as a linear model to detect overlapping regions. However, depending on the spatial distribution of subjects, a non-linear mapping may yield better results, particularly as the number of monitored cameras increases. Another promising avenue for future work is the adoption of weak supervision or semi-supervised learning, which can markedly reduce labeling effort, especially in non-stationary settings where class patterns evolve over time. To this end, a lightweight face detector can propose candidate boxes, high-confidence detections can be retained with temporal smoothing, and object trackers can propagate boxes across frames. An active-learning loop can include a human in the loop to resolve uncertain cases. For FOV boundaries, candidate lines can be estimated from motion-density heatmaps and optical-flow discontinuities, followed by targeted spot checks where uncertainty remains high. This strategy can substantially reduce annotation costs while preserving accuracy.

Our pipeline currently assumes that a subject crossing the edge will reappear in the adjacent camera, an assumption that may not hold under severe occlusion or complex multi-camera geometry.
